Du Châtelet

Mar 13, 2023

A Leibnizian problem

The phrase “labyrinth of the continuum” was prominently used by Leibniz to describe a problem which natural philosophy, in his view, needed to solve. Leibniz often writes of it as one of two labyrinths, the other one being the problem of freedom. There is a concise statement of both in the introduction to the Theodicy, though Leibniz had repeated similar statements for a long time before that:

“There are two famous labyrinths, in which our reason often gets lost: One regards the great question of the free and the necessary, in particular in the production and the origin of evil; the other consists in the discussion of continuity and of the indivisibles which seem to be its elements, a discussion which must involve the consideration of the infinite. The first labyrinth embarasses all of humanity, the other bothers only the philosophers.” 1

The labyrinth of the continuum, as Leibniz alludes in the passage, arises from the combination of two intuitions: On the one hand, for any extended entity to be continuous means that it has no determinate parts, therefore it would seem to be infinitely divisible; this is in conflict with the other intuition, namely that material wholes are constituted by their parts. Leibniz himself believed that the structure of the continuum was one of the keys to metaphysics, and that it to clear it up mathematical considerations would be central. He writes as much in a fragment from the 1670s:

„For the thread through the labyrinth of the composition of the continuum, and further of the biggest and smallest and of the indescribable and the infinite, can be provided by geometry alone; but nobody will come to a solid metaphysics who has not gone through it.”2

Wolff and Du Châtelet, like Leibniz, hold that there are indivisible, unextended substances underlying all bodily phenomena. It is therefore not surprising that they need to take a stance on the issues that Leibniz discusses under the label of the labyrinth.

Du Châtelet’s discussion of how the physical world and the visible bodies arise from the simple substances stretches over chapters 7 to 10 of the Foundations. Chapter 8 is dedicated to an investigation of the divisibility of matter specifically, and it is there that she first mentions the labyrinth of the continuum:

“The majority of philosophers, having confounded the abstraction of our mind with the physical body, wanted to prove the infinite divisibility of matter through the arguments of the geometers about the divisibility of lines that is taken to infinity, which has given occasion to that famous labyrinth of the divisibility of the continuum which has so embarrassed the philosophers. But they would have been spared all difficulties which that divisibility entails if they had taken care never to apply arguments about the divisibility of the geometrical body to natural and physical bodies.”3

Du Châtelet’s introduction of the labyrinth is clearly influenced by Leibniz: She uses his phrase almost exactly, and her use of the word “embarassed” might even be a conscious nod towards the specific passage from the Theodicy quoted above. It is therefore safe to say that she agrees that the structure of the continuum is an important problem of natural philosophy. To solve this problem, according to her, one simply needs to refrain from applying geometrical arguments to bodies. She offers no reasons, however, for why the arguments of geometry should not be applicable to physical bodies, or what exactly she takes the undesirable consequences of such a misapplication to be. In the next section, I shall turn to the views of philosopher and mathematician who articulated worries of this kind, in order to see how Du Châtelet’s account might be sharpened by the contrast.

Euler’s criticism

There was at least one near-contemporary of Du Châtelet who also thought that the difficulties about the physical continuum could not be resolved by simply drawing a distinction between geometrical and physical arguments: Leonhard Euler. Euler is best known as a mathematician, but he also wrote a popular exposition of Newtonian natural philosophy in the form of his Letters to a German Princess. In between discussions of motion theory, optics and acoustics, these letters also address more general philosophical topics, among them the relation of body and soul, the basics of epistemology, and logic. Euler’s main opponents in these philosophical sections are authors of a broadly Leibnizian type, which he calls “monadists.” One of the points that he criticizes most fervently in them is their view of the continuum.

Euler’s own position is that there are simply no indivisible parts underlying the continuum, neither spiritual nor material. He holds that anything that is extended can always be further divided, without thereby presupposing any ultimate particles.4 Euler denies, in other words, that there need to be any determinate and discrete entities in order for there to be a continuum. One of his favorite arguments for this is precisely that it is incoherent to do what Du Châtelet recommends, namely to draw a distinction between geometrical and physical divisibility:

“[…] if divisibility in infinitum is a property of extension in general, it must of necessity likewise belong to all individual extended beings; […]”5

If geometrical extension is an abstract and general notion that is instantiated in the extended physical bodies, Euler argues, then our conclusions about geometrical extension must also apply to extended bodies. By a few simple geometrical operations, it can be shown that any given line, surface or body can in principle always be divided; the same must therefore be true of the physical bodies that instantiate these abstracta. Euler then suggests that there are some contemporaries of his who deny this inference:

There are however philosophers, particularly among our contemporaries, who boldly deny, that the properties applicable to extension, in general, that is, according as we consider them in geometry, take place in bodies really existing. They allege that geometrical extension is an abstract being, from the properties of which it is impossible to draw any conclusion, with respect to real objects.” 6

From the passage in the Foundations quoted above, it would indeed seem that Du Châtelet makes the kind of distinction that Euler argues against here. As we have seen, she advises that one should take “care never to apply arguments about the divisibility of the geometrical body to natural and physical bodies.”7 If we go with Euler and take this to mean that physical objects are extended in a different sense than the objects of geometry, the claim is indeed difficult to defend. However, as we shall see shortly, Du Châtelet’s position is less radical than Euler’s caricature implies.

In the upcoming sections, I shall give an overview of the positions taken by Du Châtelet and Wolff, as well as some historical context.

Wolff & Du Châtelet on divisibility: Distinction between material atoms and atoms of nature is the way out of the labyrinth

Now that we have seen both how Euler paraphrases the monadist position on infinite divisibility and his criticism of it, let us see whether his descriptions fit what Wolff and Du Châtelet write, beginning with Wolff. Wolff’s doctrine on the divisibility of matter, in its most elaborate form, can be found in his Latin treatise on natural philosophy, the Cosmologia generalis.8 According to Wolff, there exist what he calls “material atoms,” extended particles that are not divisible by natural causes. From these material atoms, Wolff distinguishes the absolutely indivisible “atoms of nature”:

“§186. An atom of nature is what is indivisible in itself, and so lacks any parts into which it could be divided. But a material atom is called that which is divisible in itself, but for the division of which no causes that exist in nature are sufficient.”9

The material atoms are not divisible by natural means, but they are divisible in the geometrical perspective or, what amounts to the same thing, by God’s omnipotence. Wolff also calls them “primitive corpuscles” because they are the basic building blocks of material things.10 In contrast, only simple substances are absolutely indivisible atoms of nature, because only they have no extension. It is this distinction between primitive corpuscles or material atoms on the one hand and simple substances or atoms of nature on the other which according to Wolff provides the way out of the labyrinth of the continuum:

“Commonly people acknowledge nothing but derivative corpuscles. For this reason they consider every corpuscle as if it is composed out of smaller ones, and therefore do not acknowledge any mode of composition other than through other explicable corpuscles. But then they fall into the labyrinth of composition and division, from which there is no way out, owing to the fact that one never reaches a last term in division, nor a last reason in composition.”11

To Euler’s geometrical arguments, therefore, Wolff would have been able to concede that all extended bodies are divisible – provided that the dividing power is supernatural. God is able to divide even the material atoms into their constitutent simple substances. If the dividing powers are limited to natural forces, on the other hand, the primitive corpuscles are indivisible. The simple substances are not divisible even by God, but since they are without extension the geometrical argument does not apply to them.

As far as I can see, Du Châtelet’s follows Wolff quite closely on divisibility. Like Wolff (and like Leibniz), she holds the simple beings to be unextended and therefore indivisible.12 She also agrees with Euler and Wolff that the notion of absolutely indivisible extended particles is contradictory. In the original 1740 edition of the Foundations, Du Châtelets account of divisibility is contained in a single long paragraph, number 172. There, she argues that naturally indivisible particles are necessary for the explanation of nature:

“We have seen above that atoms or uncuttable parts of matter cannot be admitted as long as we regard them as simple, primitive and not reducible [to anything else], because it is not possible to give any sufficient reason for their existence. But as soon as we recognize that they have their origin in the simple beings, it is very well possible to admit them, for it is highly possible, and experience renders it highly believable, that there are in the universe a certain determined number of parts of matter that nature never resolves into their principle.”13

For the second edition in 1742, the paragraphs from §172 to the end of chapter 9 are entirely rewritten. These textual revisions have little impact on the doctrine itself; in the main, the second edition simply expresses the same ideas more clearly and with fewer ambiguities.

Infinity for us

Du Châtelet therefore seems to be very close to Wolff in her matter theory. But what exactly is her final word on infinite divisibility? The 1740 version of §175 contains a perplexing turn of phrase: Du Châtelet writes that

§175: “ in relation to us, matter is not only divisible, but divided to infinity, even though its divisions really have limits; for these limits are so remote that they extend to infinity for us, because infinity for us is a quantity that no number can express.” 14

The claim that “in relation to us,” matter is infinitely divisible and even infinitely divided would seem to be at odds with the equally prominent claim that the number of primary particles as well as the number of unextended monads is finite. But though the language of the passage just quoted might suggest that Du Châtelet supports both ultimate extended particles and infinite divisibility, the overall evidence points towards a strictly discrete account of matter.

The revisions that Du Châtelet made to chapter 9 between 1740 and 1742 help clear up the initial confusion. We have seen that in the first edition, the labyrinth of the continuum is mentioned in §168. For the second edition, the sentence containing that reference is cut from §168, and instead the labyrinth is mentioned in the new §172. The original version of that paragraph had consisted of a number of plausibility arguments for the existence of stable particles. The revised version turns it around: Du Châtelet now emphasizes that we never encounter direct evidence of uncuttable particles: “However far we push our divisions, there still remains extension.”15 This physical experience, together with the misguided application of geometry to bodies, is what leads philosophers into the “labyrinth of the composition and infinite division of the continuum.” The simple substances and the extended atoms which they constitute are the obvious solution to the paradoxes entailed by infinite divisibility (the Zenonic paradoxes are explicitly mentioned in §171). Du Châtelet is rather brief about this argument for the existence of the simple substances, since she assumes that she has conclusively proved it in §120. Here in the second version of chapter 9, she launches directly into a paraphrase of Wolff’s distinction of primary and secondary particles. She emphasizes above all how incredibly small and numerous even the derivative particles are. Among the experiments cited is the fact that a small amount of gold can be hammered into a very large leaf. The point of these illustrations is to show that matter seems like a continuous quantity, even though it is constituted by discrete, extended entites. In conclusion, the “infinite division” mentioned in the original version of §175 can only be figurative, in the sense that for any division of matter that human artifice can achieve, the primary particles are smaller still. If it is therefore Du Châtelet’s position that each physical body is composed of a finite number of naturally indivisible atoms, how can she respond to Euler’s protestations about the applicability of geometry? The answer is that she holds geometry to apply not to bodies as they truly are, but merely to bodies as they appear to us. This ideality of extension, like the basics of the doctrine of simple substances, is discussed already in chapter 7 of the Foundations. In §134, she writes that extension, like all properties belonging to composites instead of simple substances, is a phenomenon:

“[Extension] is but a phenomenon, an abstraction from multiple real things […] And so, if we were able to see distinctly all that composes extension, that appearance of extension that seems so obvious to us would disappear; and our soul would have apperceptions only of simple beings existing outside one another; just as if we were to distinguish all the little portions of matter moving in different ways that compose a portrait, that portrait, which is a phenomenon, would disappear for us.“16

Similarly, Wolff writes in the Cosmology that “extension and continuity are phenomena.”17

Both Wolff and Du Châtelet are therefore idealists about continuous extension: They hold that the objects of geometry are results of our perceptual apparatus.18 According to both of them, geometrical extension is phenomenal and as such has an infinity of possible divisions (we can imagine it divided however we want), but extended bodies consist of a finite number of extended atoms (though the number of these is so large that they are infinite for all practical purposes). With this in mind, we can appreciate the following passage, in which Du Châtelet summarizes her solution to the problems of the continuum.

§171: “for we have seen that it is proven by this principle [the PSR] that physical extension is ultimately composed of simple beings, and that consequently its divisions, even the possible ones, have real and positive limits. […] So the infinite divisibility of extension is at the same time a geometrical truth and a physical error. […] For any finite whole, for example a foot [i.e., a body one foot long], is a composite of the finite and the infinite: this foot is finite, insofar as it contains only a certain number of simple beings, but I may suppose it to be divided into an infinity [of parts], or rather into an unfinished quantity of parts, by considering that foot as an abstract extension.”19

Wolff and Du Châtelet have a very similar position on infinite divisibility. In short, they distinguish three types of entities according to divisibility. The simple beings are unextended and therefore absolutely indivisible; the primary particles are naturally indivisible, but have extension and could still be divided into their constitutent simple beings by God’s power; the derivative particles, lastly, can become actually divided in the course of nature. This system is a valid response to the kind of criticism offered by Euler (though in actuality that criticism was of course offered post facto). The geometrical arguments for infinite divisibility are deflected by relocating it to the realm of the potential: Any division of an extended quantity can be executed in the human imagination, which is sufficient for geometrical operations; any imaginable division could furthermore be actualized by God’s absolute power. Infinite divisibility in both of these senses is compatible with the existence of “primitive corpuscles,” extended particles that cannot be divided by any natural power.

A sketch of a history of corpuscularianism

At this point, I would like to give some context to the two contrasting positions of Euler on the one hand and Wolff and Du Châtelet on the other. For although I have so far limited myself to reconstructing the arguments of these three 18th-century philosophers, parts of their discussion had been common currency in natural philosophy for a long time before that. At the beginning of her chapter 7 on the “Elements of matter,” Du Châtelet gives her version of a history of doctrines on the smallest elements of material bodies. According to her, there were three main historical opinions on what these elements are. In a first step, Aristotle synthesized the thoughts of his predecessors into the system of the four elements, each of them simple in the sense of not being composed of anything of a different nature than itself. Descartes then replaced Aristotle’s four elements with his three types of corpuscles, these corpuscles being differentiated by their size and shape. Thirdly, post-cartesian natural philosophers then dropped the differentiation into three separate types of particle and simply assumed that matter is “a uniform and similar mass, without any internal difference, but the small parts of which have such different shapes and sizes that the infinite variety that reigns in this universe can result from them.”20 Du Châtelet also remarks that the particles which result from this approach are not very different from those postulated by Epicurus, since in both cases the difference between one particle and another lies in their different size and/or shape alone. The fourth phase in the development of matter theory begins with Leibniz’ insight that “these atoms did not give him the reason for the existence of the extension of matter”21 and that extension itself needed to be founded in unextended monads. The doctrine of the elements of matter, as du Châtelet proceeds to lay it out in the rest of chapter 7, is presented as a summary of Wolff’s solution to this Leibnizian problem.

What is more, it would be shortsighted to frame du Châtelet’s treatment of the questions surrounding the divisibility of matter as an issue of her relation to Leibniz (or even Wolff) alone. In what sense physical bodies might be infinitely divisible is one of the foundational questions on which anyone formulating a natural philosophy in the early 18th century needed to take a stance. To see why, it is necessary to recapitulate the stages of the mini-history of atomism given by Du Châtelet, this time with a view towards divisibility. That a continuous entity, for example a line, may be treated as infinitely divisible in geometry was commonly accepted in antiquity. Consequently, the move that du Châtelet alludes to, distinguishing mathematical from physical indivisibility, was one taken by many ancient atomists.22 They posited indivisible physical atoms first and foremost, and argued that whether geometrical lines and planes were infinitely divisible or not had no impact on the possibility of indivisible extended atoms. The atoms of Epicurus, for instance, have a certain extension and therefore can be imagined as having parts. Nevertheless, they are metaphysically indivisible, so that not even the Gods are able to divide them. Atomism in this sense played very little role among the medieval scholastics because Aristotle’s arguments against it were perceived as conclusive. History knows of a small number of medieval scholastic atomists, but they defend a mathematical atomism, according to which abstract continua are made up of unextended points. An important point of reference is book 6 of the Physics, where Aristotle argues against such a position. In any case, the understanding was that the results of the entire discussion would have little impact on the nature of physical bodies or the explanation of natural phenomena generally.23 When a growing number of writers started to discuss indivisible particles in the 16th century, they did so under terms that differed both from those of the medieval scholastics and those of the ancients. In contrast to the predominantly mathematical character of 14th-century scholastic atomism, these early modern authors were mainly interested in how one could use the hypothesis of invisibly small but extended particles in order to better explain natural phenomena. In contrast to the ancient atomists, on the other hand, very few early modern neo-atomists claimed the ultimate extended particles to be absolutely indivisible: For the early moderns, the omnipotence of the Christian God meant that any entity which could be imagined as divided could be actually divided by God. The ancient position, which combined metaphysical atomism with divisibilism in geometry, was therefore out of the question. Even though they often claim Democritus, Leucippus and Epicurus as their inspiration, the corpuscularians of the sixteenth and seventeenth century seem more interested in the usefulness of particles for explaining this or that phenomenon than in investigating the ultimate nature of matter. This lead the historian of atomism Kurd Lasswitz to locate the ancient source of early modern corpuscularians in “technical” authors like Hero of Alexandria, rather than in more metaphysically strict writers like Democritus. The degree to which the parallel questions of indivisibility in geometry and mathematics have become one by the seventeenth century is illustrated by the very treatise that coined the phrase “labyrinth of the continuum”: The Labyrinthus sive de compositione continui liber unus (1631) of Libertus Fromondus. This tract, which Leibniz knew and made extensive notes on, defends the Aristotelian view that all continua are infinitely divisible against two types of atomism. The first of these atomist views is a “pointilist” view, defended among others by Arriaga and John Major, according to which both the abstract mathematical continuum and physical extension ultimately consist of an infinite number of extended points. The second atomist view posits extended physical atoms, and Fromondus criticizes it precisely because of the absurd consequence that space itself would have to consist of indivisible parts. Fromondus gives no explicit example of anyone endorsing this second type of atomism, though the French atomist Sébastien Basson actually does embrace a strict physical atomism and draws exactly the consequences that seem so absurd to Fromondus. Basson is an exception, however, and even he seems much more interested in establishing stable corpuscles as principles of explanation in natural philosophy, rather than proving a metaphysical thesis about the structure of space. In fact, genuine metaphysical atomism is so rare in early seventeenth century Europe that one of the most remarkable aspects of the Labyrinthus is that it gives evidence that this type of atomism was present in Leuven at all by 1631. Among the early modern philosophers who took seriously the hypothesis that nature could be best explained thorugh the use of invisibly small particles, the great majority advocate not for absolutely indivisible atoms, but for merely naturally indivisible corpuscles. Though they could be divided by God at any time, these particles are not actually divided in the normal cause of nature.

In this perspective, Descartes does not do much more than continue an existing tradition, since he never advocated for absolute indivisibility either. Indeed, when he introduced his new natural philosophy, he was adamant that while it was a refutation of Aristotelianism, it was by no means a revival of ancient atomism either. Just like the earlier neo-atomists, Descartes postulated corpuscles that could be assumed to be indivisible for the purposes of physics, but which were not metaphysically indivisible. However, in contrast to other corpuscularians, for Descartes the structures of the mathematical and the physical continuum are closely related, since he identifies physical substance with extension. From his conception of res extensa, Descartes is able to infer the non-existence of the vacuum and the infinite divisibility of physical bodies.24

Despite the successes of Cartesianism, many philosophers in the generation after Descartes’ death became doubtful whether the identification of physical substance and extended thing could ever provide a sufficient foundation for natural philosophy. One alternative approach was the experimental one championed by Robert Boyle, who again emphasized the usefulness of arguments using relatively stable corpuscles over the need to make any deep metaphsical committments about their nature. Newton’s stance in this regard is quite similar to Boyle’s.

Leibniz on infinite division

A different sort of thinker, of which Leibniz is the most obvious example, argued to simply step back from the question of whether continuous magnitudes were infinitely divisible would be to leave one’s job as a natural philosopher unfinished. The specific form of the problem that Leibniz calls the labyrinth of the continuum arises out of two of his committments: To individual substances, on the one hand, and to infinite divisibility of physical bodies, on the other. In fact, Leibniz believes that physical bodies are actually infinitely divided, rather than merely divisible.

Leibniz cannot be one of Euler’s “monadists” since he advocates not only infinite divisibility, but infinite actual division of bodies. His reasoning is made most explicit in an unpublished fragment:

“Created things are actually infinite. For any body is actually divided into multiple parts, since any body is acted upon by other bodies. And any part of a body is itself a body, [which follows] from the very definition of body. And so bodies are actually infinite [in number] ôr there are more bodies than there are unities in any given number. The consequence is evident, since if an arbitrary division was given into only two parts, neglecting all others, and if of these two parts only the second one was given as subdivided, the subdivision of the second one would produce as many parts as there are divisions, as in A = L + B, B = M + C, C = N + D. It is evident that three divisions produce at least three distinct [bodies] L, M, and N.” 25

The argument seems to be the following: Let us assume that we have the smallest actual division, that is, a finite number of extended bodies that are not actually divided any further. In this situation, each of these smallest bodies would be acted on by other bodies, and therefore be separated into at least two parts. The result is a further division into even smaller bodies, ergo the original division was not the furthest one possible. One principle used by Leibniz here is that any physical interaction results in the relative motion of two parts of matter, which are thereby individuated into separate bodies. To be actually divided is nothing else except to be acted on by multiple other bodies. It is an important principle of Cartesian physics, and in this respect Leibniz is merely following Descartes. That bodies are actually infinitely divided was also upheld by Leibniz in his publicly known works. In paragraph 66 of the Monadology, for example, he writes:

“Each portion of matter is not only divisible to infinity, as the ancients recognized, but even actually subdivided without end, each part again into parts, each one of which has its motion; otherwise, it would be impossible for each portion to express the entire universe.”26

Extension as real phenomenon

We have discussed above how Euler’s accusation that the monadists make an unjustified or incoherent distinction between geometrical and physical extension is not dangerous to them. They can successfully parry that blow by allowing for different types of indivisible elements: Atoms of nature are indivisible from a physical perspective, while the unextended atoms of nature are indivisible from a metaphysical perspective as well. However, this hierarchy of indivisibles does not solve all problems. In particular, it remains unclear how the extended atoms of nature, however small they may be, can arise from the extensionless simple elements. And in fact, this point is raised by Euler as well. He begins by argueing that since, as shown by the geometrical arguments, extended atoms are impossible, the only indivisible particles that can even be coherently conceived must be without extension.

“Whoever is disposed to deny this property of extension [i.e., its infinite divisibility], is under the necessity of maintaining, that it is possible to arrive at last at parts so minute as to be unsusceptible of any farther division, because they ceased to have any extension.”27

According to Euler, then, there are only two alternatives: Either bodies are infinitely divisible (with no simple substances constituting them) or there is a limit to division, namely the unextended simple substances. The latter position is what Euler attributes to the monadists:

“[The monadists] insist, that this division extends only to a certain point, and that you may come at length to particles so minute, that, having no magnitude, they are no longer divisible. These ultimate particles, which enter into the composition of bodies, they denominate simple beings, and monads.”28

As the two passages just quoted illustrate, Euler assumes that there is some finite number of divisions with which it is possible to divide an extended thing into extensionless monads. He then has no difficulties in showing that a finite number of unextended parts cannot constitute extended bodies, arguing along the following lines: Assume that after a body with a length of one inch into one thousand parts, one arrives at indivisibles; since these are necessarily unextended points, the length of the original body is a thousand times zero, which is still zero. 29 What the “monadists” have to do in order to evade arguments like those of Euler is to deny that the relation of extended bodies and unextended indivisibles is a relation of part and wholes.

Since we know at least in Du Châtelet’s case that there is a finite number of simple beings in a single atom, the only way out is to claim that the simple beings are something other than parts of the atom, that they constitute it in some other way.

Extension from difference

So all the monadists are in agreement that extension does not have substantial being, but is a phenomenon founded in the simple beings. But how exactly do the simple beings found the phenomenon of extension?

In §§215-221 of Wolff’s Cosmology, we find an argument that could be called “Extension from difference.” Wolff starts out by establishing that his simple substances are not what he calls “zenonic points.” Zenonic points are simply points in the geometrical sense: unextended but located in a specific place. Because such points have no individuating properties, they can only be distinguished from one another if an external frame of reference is assumed. In contrast, each simple substance is distinguishes from all others by its internal properties. From this, Wolff infers that the simple substances must exist outside one another:

§219: The elements of material things exist outside one another. For the elements of material things are dissimilar each from the other (§195), and so one is distinct from the other (§211 Ontol.) Therefore, each exists outside of the other, ôr all of them outside one another.“30

§221: The aggregates of the elements are extended. For when elements are aggregated, they exist outside one another (§219) and are united with each other (§220). […]Therefore, since that in which diverse [things], that is, [things] existing outside one another, are united must be extended, the aggregates of the elements are extended.”31

“It is also through this dissimilarity that it is possible to understand how non-extended beings can form extended beings; and how the sufficient reason of the extension of matter is found in the simple beings, for the elements exist necessarily outside one another (sinceone can never be the other), and since they are all united with and linked to one another, as we have just seen, there results a compilation of multiple diverse beings that all exist outside one another and which are one whole in virtue of their connection; but I have shown that we cannot represent extension to ourselves in any other way than as a compilation of diverse coexisting things that exist outside one another; and so, the Leibnizians conclude that aggregate of simple beings must be extended.”32

I.e., Simple beings are distinct, but linked.
